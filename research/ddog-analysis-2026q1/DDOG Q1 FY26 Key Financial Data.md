## Cloud Infrastructure Dependency (FY2025 10-K, Risk Factors — Item 1A)

- Datadog does **not** name a specific cloud infrastructure provider (e.g., AWS/Azure/GCP) anywhere
  in the 10-K's discussion of its own hosting dependency. The relevant risk factor states: "We
  outsource substantially all of the infrastructure relating to our cloud solution to third-party
  hosting services," and warns that a termination, service lapse, or capacity limitation at "third-
  party hosting services" could require "re-architecting our cloud solution for deployment on a
  different cloud infrastructure service provider" — language that implies reliance on a limited
  number of providers without naming or quantifying concentration. **Confidence: low** — this is
  boilerplate risk-factor language, not a disclosed single-vendor dependency figure.
- Separately, in the Competition section, the 10-K names AWS, Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud
  Platform (GCP) as competitors "with respect to cloud monitoring" via their native tooling — i.e.,
  the same hyperscalers Datadog may depend on for hosting are also named as competitors in a specific
  product category. **Confidence: high** (direct quote).
- No customer-concentration disclosure (e.g., "no customer accounted for more than 10% of revenue")
  appears in the 10-Q or 10-K — consistent with a base of ~33,200 customers where the largest
  disclosed cohort ($100k+ ARR) still represents only ~4,550 accounts. This is a structural contrast
  with the "higher degree of conservatism to our largest customer" guidance language above — the
  *concentration risk* being hedged against is not disclosed as a formal 10-K risk factor.

  price of $195.63–$202.58 across several tranches — explicitly footnoted as **"shares sold pursuant
  to a 10b5-1 plan dated December 15, 2025"**, i.e., scheduled roughly five months before the print,
  not a discretionary sale timed to the earnings pop. Amit Agarwal also sold ~20,000 shares the same
  day at similar prices.
- **2026-05-12**: additional smaller routine Form 4s (Amit Agarwal; Michael James Callahan).

Every reviewed transaction in this window is coded as a scheduled 10b5-1-plan sale following routine
option vesting/exercise, not an open-market discretionary purchase or sale. The pattern (staggered
sales across narrow price bands, pre-committed plan dates well before the print) is consistent with
automated selling programs rather than opportunistic trading on non-public information. No Form 4
shows a cluster of *unusually large or newly-adopted* selling activity concentrated in the 1–2 trading
days immediately preceding the May 7 print that would read as a stronger red flag; the CEO's largest
single sale (127,141 shares, ~$25M notional) came four days *after* the print, under a plan adopted in
December 2025.
