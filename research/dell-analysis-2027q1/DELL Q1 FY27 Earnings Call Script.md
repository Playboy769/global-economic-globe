# Dell Technologies Inc. (DELL) Q1 FY2027 Earnings Call Transcript

**Powered by Quartr** — May 28, 2026, 4:30 PM EDT — Earnings call

Dell delivered record Q1 FY27 results with revenue up 88% YoY to $43.8B and EPS up 214% to
$4.86, driven by exceptional AI server demand and broad-based growth across all segments.
Management raised full-year revenue guidance by $27B to $165–$169B and expects continued
margin expansion, though supply constraints—primarily in memory—will limit upside despite
robust demand.

## Q&A

**Ben Reitzes (Melius Research)** — Pull-forward vs. sustainable demand; puts and takes behind
the raised second-half guide across major segments.

Jeff Clarke: Several demand factors — pull-in/buy-ahead on price concerns, large PC/server
install-base refresh (1/3 of PCs 4+ years old, majority of servers on 14G or older), new AI-driven
demand for traditional servers (inference, agentic), and share gains across all four segments.
Customers seek a "calming hand" from Dell during supply disruption. Forward pipelines are
healthier than historical norms, supporting the $27B FY guide raise.

**Mark Newman (Bernstein)** — Units vs. pricing breakdown for the traditional server/PC
outperformance; sustainability beyond 1-2 quarters.

Jeff Clarke: Absolute unit growth in PCs (share gains, especially high price band) and servers
(plus content growth — more cores/DRAM/NAND per unit), layered with inflationary pricing.
Declined to break out the exact unit/price split. AI "drag" is pulling dense traditional servers
into neoclouds and advanced enterprise inference/agentic use cases.

**Amit Daryanani (Evercore)** — Guide implies ~48% of FY revenue in H2 vs. a historical ~52%;
how much of that shift is pull-in vs. deliberate conservatism vs. component availability?

Jeff Clarke: "I'm the problem. We have a supply issue... not a demand issue." David Kennedy:
demand outpaces supply broadly (GPU, CPU, traditional server, PC); team will keep matching
supply to demand and chasing the pipeline into H2.

**Wamsi Mohan (Bank of America)** — Does current demand pull forward next year's IT budgets?
Materiality of agentic-driven traditional server demand to Tier 2 CSPs as an offset to any
enterprise demand deceleration.

Jeff Clarke: "I can't speak to next year and budgets for customers." Multi-year (3-5 year)
supply-access conversations underway with large customers; ~2-quarter forward pipeline
visibility is unusually strong. David Kennedy: expects to exit the year with meaningful backlog;
highlighted double-digit DFS financing origination growth across all business lines helping
customers manage budgets while still taking delivery.

**Katherine Murphy (Goldman Sachs)** — Where across the 5,000-customer base is the $10B
incremental AI server FY guidance coming from? How much capacity can current manufacturing
partners support?

David Kennedy: Backlog $51.3B; FY AI guide raised $10B just 90 days into the year; growth
broad-based across neoclouds/sovereigns/enterprise; customer count up >50% in six months;
5-quarter forward pipeline is "multiples" of backlog. Jeff Clarke / David Kennedy on capacity:
"there's no capacity issue, it's parts" / "supply."

**Samik Chatterjee (JPMorgan)** — Is the improved ex-AI gross margin outlook driven by price
increases or product mix?

David Kennedy: Primarily Dell IP storage mix (PowerStore, unstructured/PowerScale) driving
array-level margin tailwind; traditional server and CSG margins sustained through pricing
discipline.

**Asiya Merchant (Citigroup)** — Attach rate of storage/services to AI servers; is the Dell IP
storage uptick attach-driven; confirm AI server margin target.

Jeff Clarke: Extensive color on storage momentum (PowerMax 5 quarters of growth, PowerStore 9,
PowerScale 4, ObjectScale 3, data protection 2), unstructured storage as "the data set that
feeds the beast" in AI, Lightning parallel file system, PowerStore Elite specs. No explicit
attach-rate percentage given.

**Erik Woodring (Morgan Stanley)** — Knowing what you know now (agentic, share gains), how would
you revise the October Analyst Day 3-year targets (7-9% revenue growth, 15%+ EPS growth)?

David Kennedy: "I don't think we'd work a five-year program on the Q1 earnings call" — declined
to update; pointed to the Q1/Q2 guide and ~2.5-quarter pipeline visibility instead. Jeff Clarke:
extended framing of agentic AI as a new TAM ("the CPU as the harness that supports every AI
agent"); explicitly: "I can't tell you how big the TAM is... we're in the early innings."

**David Vogt (UBS)** — How was CSG operating margin (~8%, best in years) achieved? Price vs.
low-cost inventory? Long-term trend back to Investor Day range?

Jeff Clarke: OpEx leverage (600bps YoY, 300bps QoQ) was the single biggest lever; admits pricing
was moved "purposely... a little too early in retrospect," which tempered transactional
(consumer/SMB) demand; TRU mix uplift plus peripherals/services attach also contributed;
"we are not operating at COVID margins... we don't have it perfect yet."

**Tim Long (Barclays)** — Given traditional server FY guide (~60%) vastly outpaces storage
(mid-single-digit), could storage see a longer growth tail later? Does repeated repricing get
harder if commodity costs rise further?

David Kennedy: Storage guide is dampened by the ongoing Dell-IP-vs-third-party mix crossover,
which "by the end of this year... stops becoming any relevant element of our bridge." Jeff
Clarke on pricing: "repricing it feels like every day"; expects the inflationary environment
(fuel, raw materials, DRAM, NAND, CPUs) to persist; some customers "wait it out" once pricing is
high enough, others accelerate buying to lock in supply.

**Simon Leopold (Raymond James)** — Beyond memory, what else constrains upside (PCBs, etc.)?
Rank-order the constraints.

Jeff Clarke: DRAM/NAND first, then microprocessors (CPUs), then hard drives, then a broader
component tail; leading-edge semiconductor nodes are "fully allocated" with ~1-year lead times;
trailing nodes increasingly filling up too.

**Krish Sankar (TD Cowen)** — Mix of x86 vs. Arm in servers; does architecture affect margin?

Jeff Clarke: Traditional servers are 100% x86 today. Large-scale liquid-cooled AI racks
(GB200/GB300, moving to Vera Rubin) skew Arm; "Enterprise AI" (B200/B300, RTX 6000 Pro) skews
x86. No margin-differential commentary given.
