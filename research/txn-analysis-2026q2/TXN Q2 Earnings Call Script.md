## Prepared Remarks

**Mike Beckman, Head of Investor Relations** — Welcomed listeners, noted forward-looking statement disclosures, introduced CEO Haviv Ilan, CFO Rafael Lizardi, and incoming CFO Julie Knecht (effective Aug 1; 25+ years at TI, most recently Chief Accounting Officer since 2021). Rafael Lizardi, CFO for nearly a decade, is retiring at the end of August — this is his final earnings call.

**Haviv Ilan, CEO** — Q2 revenue $5.5B, +13% QoQ, +23% YoY. Analog +26% YoY, Embedded Processing +16% YoY, Other segment -2% YoY. Revenue came in above range on continued industrial/data center growth plus accelerated automotive growth. By end market: Industrial +~30% YoY, +~10% QoQ (broad across sectors/regions); Automotive +mid-teens YoY, +upper-single-digits QoQ; Data center doubled YoY, +~20% QoQ; Personal electronics flat YoY, +upper-single-digits QoQ; Communications equipment grew YoY and QoQ.

**Rafael Lizardi, CFO** — Gross profit $3.4B (61% of revenue), +340bps QoQ. OpEx $1.0B (as expected); TTM OpEx $3.9B (20% of revenue). Operating profit $2.3B (42% of revenue), +48% YoY. Net income $2.0B, EPS $2.14 (includes $0.05 benefit from discrete tax items, not in original guidance). Cash flow from operations $2.7B in quarter, $8.7B TTM. CapEx $514M in quarter, $3.3B TTM. Free cash flow TTM $6.5B (up from $1.8B in Q2 2025); TTM FCF includes $1.6B of CHIPS Act incentives (ITC + direct funding); received $549M of ITC-related payments in Q2 for qualifying capex. Paid $1.3B in dividends in the quarter; returned $5.8B to owners over trailing 12 months. Cash and short-term investments $7B at quarter end. Total debt $14B, weighted average coupon 4%. Inventory $4.6B, down $90M sequentially; days 196, down 13 days sequentially.

**Q3 2026 guidance:** Revenue $5.65B–$6.15B; EPS $2.23–$2.57; effective tax rate ~13%.

Rafael closed with remarks on his 25-year TI tenure and confidence in the company's future.

## Q&A

**Harlan Sur (J.P. Morgan)** — Asked whether Q2's above-seasonal strength (particularly automotive inflection) continues into Q3/H2, and what's driving automotive. Haviv: broader demand setup vs. prior quarters (was industrial/data-center-led, now automotive joining); Q3 guide contribution from all three growth markets plus seasonal PE strength; automotive inflection built through the quarter, led by China EV/hybrid demand (fuel cost driven) plus customer inventory at unsustainably low levels. Follow-up on pricing: confirmed H1 pricing was flat/stable as predicted (an above-average outcome vs. typical annual price declines); has started executing price increases, customer-by-customer (not a single distributor discussion), some effect in Q3 continuing into Q4; some customers' pricing set annually at year-end Q4 discussions, which can carry into next year; pressure/lead-time escalation currently concentrated in analog, with embedded "joining the trend"; embedded pricing discussions more centered on next year.

**Jim Schneider** — Asked about factory loadings and desired inventory position (down slightly this quarter) — stabilize or decline further? Haviv: inventory helped meet quickly-materializing demand this quarter; loadings are about preparing for the future. Julie Knecht: loadings increased Q1→Q2 and through Q2; Q3 loadings depend on demand; clean room space available to equip for a wide range of scenarios. Follow-up: given FCF strength, on pace to exceed the capital management day FCF framework? Haviv: reiterated framework unchanged — ~$8-9B FCF at $20B revenue, ~$9-10B FCF at $22B revenue — "you can use it as you model the company."

**Stacy Rasgon (Bernstein)** — Asked whether OpEx is typically down seasonally into Q3 and whether that holds this year. Rafael: OpEx, net other income/expense, and acquisition charges should all be flat Q2→Q3. Follow-up on personal electronics: given memory dynamics, is strength expected in Q3? Haviv: PE typically grows nicely in Q3 (sometimes mid-teens), expects sequential growth this year but "maybe at a lower level"; PE was flat YoY in Q2; seeing shortage-related pressure on customers; this cycle's growth is broader than just PE. Stacy pressed twice for a clear flat-YoY-vs-down-YoY answer for Q3; Mike Beckman declined to give a market-level guide, reiterating Haviv's Q2 characterization.

**Atif Malik (Citi)** — Asked about data center sales (doubled YoY) and competitive position as the industry transitions to 800V architectures. Haviv: expects continued strong data center demand; 800V phases in via multiple conversion stages (AC→800V→48V→12V, eventually direct 800V→12V/6V), which raises TAM and conversion-stage count — a tailwind for analog/embedded content across every stage. Follow-up: will data center segment gross margins converge to corporate average over time? Mike Beckman/management: roughly similar to corporate average so far, declined to skew either direction, noted new capacity is on 300mm assets with strong fall-through.

**Vivek Arya (BofA)** — Asked where TI is in the industrial recovery cycle and how many more above-seasonal quarters to expect; also asked to quantify pricing's contribution to Q3's ~8% sequential growth and whether prices can rise again in Q4. Haviv: still ~5-6 points below the 2022 industrial peak; views industrial TAM growth as mid-to-high-single-digit trend line, implying room to run; data center is providing a tailwind to industrial (energy infrastructure, test & measurement were fastest-growing sectors in H1); customers are early in inventory building, so setup is positive. On pricing: Q3 forecast is "the vast majority... just unit growth and maybe a little contribution from pricing, but it's almost insignificant." Follow-up: what's the right long-term data center growth rate (40%? 50%?) beyond 2026-2028, and does that expose TI to winning/losing specific GPU/ASIC sockets? Haviv declined to give a number, deferring to Mike Beckman, who described power-tree and signal-chain content growing with data center buildout without quantifying a growth rate; objective framed as "outgrow the market," which management believes will hold in 2026 and into 2027.

**Timothy Arcuri (UBS)** — Asked whether TI will land at the low end of its $2-3B gross CapEx guide for 2026, and whether ITC will keep contributing at the same per-quarter rate as H1 (net CapEx ~zero in H1 due to ITC). Rafael: CapEx guide unchanged at $2-3B, would not skew low — if anything could be toward the higher end given demand; ITC can be "choppy" since it applies to equipment placed in service the prior year, so H1's ITC-heavy quarter shouldn't be extrapolated; going forward, a disproportionate share of CapEx will go to assembly/test operations outside the U.S., which don't qualify for ITC; will continue to get 35% ITC on U.S. manufacturing front-end. Follow-up on data center: with high-catalog/ASSP breadth and available inventory/capacity, is TI able to exploit "hotspot" shortages to gain share, or is that the exception? Haviv: likes the complexity/diversity of rack sockets (both ASSPs and catalog parts); real-time examples of "we're lined down, please help us," which becomes a customer-trust opportunity and factors into TI's plan to keep capacity/clean-room inventory ahead of demand.

**Joshua Buchalter (TD Cowen)** — Noted flat OpEx implies roughly flat Q3 gross margin despite higher volume; asked to walk through GM puts/takes (pricing, loadings, 300mm mix) vs. depreciation offset. Julie Knecht: with OpEx/acquisition charges/net OIE flat, apply a 70-85% fall-through (ex-depreciation) to get in the right range; loadings depend on revenue but that model gets you close. Rafael interjected: "It shouldn't be flat. It should be a little higher." Follow-up on capacity/CapEx runway (Sherman/Lehi shells built out) and early 2027 CapEx read: Haviv: clean room readiness is a major improvement vs. the last cycle (previously the biggest headache, 2-3 years to equip); equipping Richardson/RFAB2 at a high level; full clean room available in Sherman 1, a second shell in Sherman 2; Lehi 2 shell arriving by year-end (Lehi 1 benefited from Stellaris manufacturing transfer from foundries, no new customer qualifications needed for Lehi 2); "in great shape" on brick-and-mortar for analog growth; declined specific numbers, deferring to the February capital management call; already making CapEx decisions with 2027/2028 (and beyond) in mind. Rafael added: use the 1.2x-revenue-growth capital-intensity framework as a rule of thumb for CapEx.

**Tom O'Malley (Barclays)** — Asked for color on out-quarter strength by segment/vector, and whether "just-in-time to just-in-case" inventory behavior continues to drive above-seasonal growth. Haviv: demand signal is strong and broad "everywhere"; deferred detailed color to the October call; team executed well in Q2, expects the same in Q3. Follow-up: what's the trigger/metric for deciding to expand physical capacity footprint vs. raising utilization on existing facilities? Haviv: reiterated more color to come at the 2027 capital management call; "good for the next three years" using existing footprint (Richardson, Sherman 1 + Sherman 2 shell, Lehi 1 + Lehi 2) in "phase three: modulate the equipment"; also thinking about 2029 and beyond.

**William Stein (Truist)** — Asked for an update on Silicon Labs acquisition timing/terms/approvals. Julie Knecht: regulatory approvals moving as planned; still expect to close in H1 next year (2027); financing plan unchanged — cash on hand plus debt. Follow-up on backlog duration and whether lead-time quotes are extending. Mike Beckman: backlog built through Q2, both immediate-shipment orders and further-out backlog; reflected in the Q3 guide. Haviv added: lead times remain competitive; were "below 13 weeks" core in Q2 (referenced from a prior conference fireside chat), now ticking up "a couple of weeks" due to growing demand, though not dramatically; TI's lead times still viewed as most competitive in the market.

**Tore Svanberg (Stifel)** — Asked whether TI, better prepared this cycle (capacity, low lead times) than last cycle, is seeing more customers come to it because of those low lead times (share-gain signal). Haviv: share moves slowly and is measured over time; competitors quoting up to 52-week lead times create opportunity for TI (less customer visibility → some restart designs even on complex parts); believes TI gained some share last year; needs to let 2026 play out; expects this cycle to provide more opportunity, to be assessed "when we get to the peak." Follow-up: what's really driving industrial strength — pricing, customer inventory levels, indirect AI tailwind, or higher ASPs from newer/higher-value products? Mike Beckman: broad strength across sector base and region base in Q2 and H1; four years of secular content growth in industrial automation; aerospace/defense, energy infrastructure (AI-adjacent), and robotics grew at higher rates; every sector and region grew both sequentially and YoY; attributed more to secular content growth than pricing (H1 pricing stable/flat, any pricing benefit would show in Q3+). Haviv added: driven by secular growth — customers depleted inventory of finished goods from the prior cycle's overbuild, and once depletion ends, new systems (designed over the last 4-5 years, given long industrial design cycles) drive demand; content growth generation-to-generation is significant; believes TI grew share on a larger content opportunity, to be validated over time; sees industrial as directionally similar to automotive in this respect.

**Haviv Ilan (closing remarks)** — TI's core identity is engineering/technology; the long-term objective and best metric for shareholder value is free cash flow per share growth over the long term.
