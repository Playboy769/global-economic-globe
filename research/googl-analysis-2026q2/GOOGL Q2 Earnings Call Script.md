# Alphabet Inc. (GOOG/GOOGL) — Q2 FY2026 Earnings Call Transcript

Source: Quartr / provided by user. Call date: Jul 22, 2026, 4:30 PM EDT.
Participants: Sundar Pichai (CEO), Philipp Schindler (SVP & Chief Business Officer), Anat Ashkenazi (CFO), Jim Friedland (Head of IR).

## Summary (per source)
Alphabet delivered a robust Q2 2026 with revenue up 24% YoY to $119.8B and operating income up 30% to $40.8B, driven by AI-led growth across Search, YouTube, and Cloud. Cloud revenue surged 82% YoY to $24.8B, with backlog reaching $514B, while management raised full-year CapEx guidance to $195B-$205B and signaled continued supply constraints and margin pressures ahead.

Other Bets revenue $382M, operating loss $1.8B. Alphabet-level activities operating loss $5.8B (shared AI R&D).

### Outlook (Q3 2026 and FY2026)
- FX: slight headwind expected in Q3 (vs +1pp tailwind in Q2), mainly in Search/YouTube ads.
- Google Services: continued AI-driven innovation; will begin lapping Q3-2025 Search acceleration.
- Google Cloud: strong demand expected to continue; TPU system sale revenue recognition still a relatively small portion of total agreement value this year, ramping into year-end; vast majority of TPU agreement revenue expected in 2027.
- Given supply-constrained environment, plans to expand use of third-party capacity in Q3 as a bridging strategy while building out internal capacity — keeps growing customer base/captures value but creates modest near-term Cloud margin pressure.
- CapEx: FY2026 guidance raised to $195B–$205B (from $180B–$190B), due to accelerated delivery of capacity to meet demand. 2027 CapEx expected to increase significantly (more detail later).
- Expenses: continued pressure from higher depreciation + data center opex (energy); continued hiring in AI/Cloud; continued marketing investment in AI products.
- FCF: expected to remain under pressure from technical infrastructure investment.

## Q&A

**1. Brian Nowak (Morgan Stanley)** — (a) How has the view on the size of the overall GenAI ROIC opportunity and the timing of that ROIC changed vs a year ago? (b) Forward CapEx budgeting philosophy/constraints for 2027 given supply constraints.
- Sundar: "very early innings" of a secular shift; core consumer business — end-to-end agentic experiences; enterprise side — CEOs "barely scratching the early stages"; cloud-adoption analogy (AI-native workload penetration still low); full-stack approach; "more bullish... than a year ago."
- Anat: goal is to invest as long as attractive return exists; take a multi-year view plus near-term build; demand still outpaces the capacity added over the past three years; full-stack approach drives operational/technological efficiencies to deliver more compute per dollar.

**2. Doug Anmuth (JPMorgan)** — (a) Confidence Gemini models remain at the frontier given Google's lower release cadence/noise vs other labs; plans for coding/agentic coding. (b) Capital structure — optimal mix and cost of debt vs equity after recent raises.
- Sundar: frontier is "incredibly dynamic"; acknowledges coding/agentic coding is an area needing improvement, team focused on it; Flash is the workhorse (performance/cost/reliability/latency sweet spot) used across cybersecurity, data analytics, customer service, professional services; 3.6 Flash vs 3.5 Flash: >10pt jump on DeepSuite benchmark, more token-efficient; testing with customers; will need a larger base model (Gemini 4, in training) to compete at the next frontier level; hints at near-monthly release cadence going forward.
- Anat: funding waterfall — (1) cash from operations first, (2) debt, (3) equity; debt portfolio expanded from ~$16B a year ago to ~$100B across currencies/geographies; equity raise done for balance-sheet resilience; not planning to return to equity markets except the ATM program to cover SBC-related tax obligations.

**3. Eric Sheridan (Goldman Sachs)** — (a) Key learnings scaling TPU efforts — demand, and balancing external TPU demand vs internal/custom-silicon needs over the next couple years. (b) More granularity on TPU's share of the $514B Cloud backlog and revenue recognition/margin impact in years ahead.
- Sundar: pleased with TPU roadmap; first priority is allocating compute for frontier/AGI development; uses both TPUs and GPUs mainly to serve models (Vertex AI, Gemini Enterprise) and agentic workloads; balances external infrastructure demand by putting TPUs in customer/partner data centers (cites the Blackstone project) — used to balance frontier-model allocation against serving consumer/enterprise business.
- Anat: TPU system sale agreements ARE reflected in the $514B backlog, but the vast majority of backlog is GCP agreements; inventory is built ahead of delivery (visible in operating cash flow); revenue recognized once systems are delivered — this quarter was a small amount; will ramp through 2026; vast majority of agreement revenue comes in 2027.

**4. Ross Sandler (Barclays)** — Speed of model releases / how to accelerate cadence vs other labs (referencing the third-party compute deal with SpaceX); is the low-cost Flash end of the market too crowded to be the right place to compete?
- Sundar: "parade of frontier" — best models at every price point (Flash-Lite, Flash, Pro) is deliberate strategy, not just cost positioning; committed to being at the full parade of frontier; on cadence — announced 3.5 Flash at I/O, followed by 3.6 Flash, continued iteration expected; heavy compute/effort going into Gemini 4 as the next frontier baseline; picking up pace toward "almost a monthly cadence" as part of the roadmap alongside Gemini 4.

**5. Michael Nathanson (MoffettNathanson)** — (a) What are Alphabet's moats in the model war if everyone converges on similar model capability? (b) TPU margin impact — incremental to core Cloud margin, or lower margin?
- Sundar: full-stack solutions where the model is "just an ingredient" (cybersecurity/Chronicle+Wiz+CodeMender, data analytics/Gemini Enterprise); even model-consumption use cases are becoming end-to-end orchestrated agentic systems requiring compute + data quality + confidentiality guarantees + secure provisioning at scale; owning models lets Google optimize integrated solutions while also offering other models/infrastructure.
- Anat: doesn't break out margins by product/infrastructure component; frames TPU system sales as TAM expansion; Cloud margin expanded to 35.6% this quarter (strong operational discipline + top-line leverage); Q3/rest-of-year: third-party bridging capacity will pressure Cloud operating margins; Wiz integration remains a headwind.

**6. Mark Shmulik (Bernstein)** — Is the third-party capacity bridging concentrated in a particular constraint, or broad-based? How is compute allocated across Search vs model training vs GCP — has the framework changed?
- Sundar: baseline is what's needed for frontier/AGI development; beyond that, prioritizes core products (Search, YouTube) and Cloud; within Cloud, prioritizes compute to serve Vertex/Gemini Enterprise and core solutions (data analytics, cybersecurity); on bridging deals — very large Cloud customers being supported through "an extraordinary moment"; short-term cost over a few months can be high, but highly ROI-positive over the multi-year life of the deal as more internal capacity comes online; frames it as a willingness to take an upfront cost for a multi-year opportunity with attractive margins/returns.

**7. Ron Josey (Citi)** — How are advertisers leveraging greater personalization/targeting to drive ROAS across Search and YouTube; what's driving the all-time-high in Search queries at Alphabet's scale (17% growth)?
- Philipp: 17% Search & Other growth driven by "many parts of the business working well together" + deep Gemini integration; all major verticals contributed, retail the largest, followed by finance, tech, media/entertainment; Gemini supercharges intent-matching across the whole ads infrastructure (ads quality, advertiser tools, new AI ad experiences); AI Max unlocks billions of net-new, previously non-monetizable searches; YouTube ads growth from direct response + brand, living room momentum continuing, ads roadmap ahead on both brand and DR sides (Demand Gen, Shorts); continued shoppable-format innovation in the living room to drive retail strength.

**8. Ken Gawrelski (Wells Fargo)** — (a) How do forecasted returns on 2027 compute capacity compare with 2025/2026 given supply-chain constraints and cost inflation? (b) What factors would Alphabet weigh in considering a change to Waymo's corporate structure (e.g., spinning it outside Alphabet)?
- Sundar: operates on a "discipline ROIC framework"; rising input costs are reflected in pricing/ROIC planning; 2027 capacity investment plans are supported by strong demand indicators, including renewing long-term deals with exceptional forward demand; dynamics "look healthier" than a year ago, which supports the confidence to invest.
- On Waymo: reiterates focus is on executing/scaling the business; Alphabet's bet structure and long-term investment horizon are "hugely valuable" to Waymo's ability to plan long-term; does not engage with the hypothetical conditions for a structural change — stays on "focused on scaling right now."

**9. Shweta Khajuria (Wolfe Research)** — (a) Longer-term TPU strategy — will Alphabet build a merchant-silicon business (TPU sales) with an accompanying software stack? (b) What could drive an acceleration in YouTube's ad revenue growth rate going forward?
- Sundar: TPUs already power a lot of Cloud serving; sees strong demand for TPUs as standalone systems from other customers too; industry-wide supply constraints; allocation follows the previously stated principles (frontier development first, then consumer/enterprise serving); declines to project further out — "don't want to project out too far into the future" — will scale based on opportunity/demand within existing constraints.
- Philipp (YouTube growth drivers): CTV/living-room brand opportunities; tools to help advertisers find creators, scale across screens, measure results; new/returning exclusive creator shows; Demand Gen + Shorts to expand SMB/vertical reach with less-disruptive, shorter ad formats; continued shoppable-format innovation in the living room (retail strength); Buy with Google Pay (2-click CTV checkout); Affiliate Partnership Boost (incremental sales + creator earnings via YouTube Shopping affiliate commissions).
